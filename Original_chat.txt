Task 1: The Tests & How They Differ
What Are These Assessments Called?
The tests designed specifically to help students choose career directions are called Career Interest Assessments or Vocational Interest Inventories. The most well-known ones are:

Holland's RIASEC / Self-Directed Search (SDS) — categorises people into 6 personality types (Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional) and maps them to careers
Strong Interest Inventory (SII) — one of the oldest and most research-backed career interest tools
Career Values Scale — measures what someone values in work (money, helping others, creativity, etc.)
Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) — personality-based, often used alongside career guidance
Kuder Career Interests Assessment — designed specifically for students
CareerDirect — popular in South African school counselling contexts

Your questions answered
1. How do API keys get configured — and can you change them manually?
Yes, absolutely — and that's exactly what the admin panel above shows. Here's how it works technically: API keys are never written into the website's code. Instead, they live in one row of your SYSTEM_CONFIG database table, stored in an encrypted format. When the website needs to call Anthropic (or PayFast, or SendGrid), it fetches the key from that table at runtime. This means you can log into your admin panel, click "Change" next to any key, paste in the new one, and it is live immediately — no developer needed, no redeployment, no downtime. The audit log records who changed what and when, so you always have a trail.
2. Can all the plugins/integrations be changed manually in future?
Yes — every external integration is configured exactly the same way through the admin panel. The three frameworks we built into the assessment
1. Holland's RIASEC Model (Section 1 — 15 questions)
The oldest and most research-backed career interest framework in existence, developed by John Holland in 1959 and refined continuously since. It categorises people into six personality types — Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, and Conventional — and maps those types to occupational environments. It underpins almost every serious career assessment tool used globally today, including the Strong Interest Inventory, the Self-Directed Search, and South Africa's own CareerDirect system. The HPCSA recognises it. Universities use it. It has over 60 years of peer-reviewed validation behind it.
2. Career Values Inventory (Section 2 — 7 questions)
Based on established career values research, particularly Super's Work Values Inventory and the Minnesota Importance Questionnaire. It identifies what a person needs from work to feel satisfied — whether that is creativity, helping others, financial reward, status, or autonomy. Values are what determine long-term career satisfaction even when interest and aptitude are present, so this is a critical layer that many cheaper assessments skip entirely.
3. Self-Reported Aptitude Screening (Section 3 — 23 questions)
This is the one that needs the most honest qualification. True aptitude testing — like the Differential Aptitude Test (DAT) used in SA schools — requires controlled, timed, supervised conditions with objectively scored questions. You cannot do that online unsupervised. What our Section 3 does instead is ask students to self-report their perceived strengths across numerical, verbal, spatial, interpersonal, and creative domains. This is closer to a personality inventory than a true aptitude test, and it is used to weight the career matches rather than to definitively measure ability.
